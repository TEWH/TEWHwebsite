About Texas Engineering World Health

Welcome!

Who are we?

Texas Engineering World Health is a student organization that operates at the University of Texas in the beautiful city of Austin, Texas. Though the name implies a strong correlation to Engineering, we are open to ALL majors of the University of Texas. In fact, we have outlets for those who are interested in business, science, programming, hardware, and general tinkering.

What do we do?

Well, for starters, we meet MONDAYS at 6:00 in the BME seminar room (see the members section for the most up-to-date meeting information). In Texas Engineering World Health, members study current global health issues and utilize their knowledge and skills to create tangible products to address these problems. Although the main focus of the organization is geared towards design project competitions, we also have workshops and tutorials where we teach our members skills such as practical circuit design, Solidworks, manufacturing, business fundamentals, etc.

What design competition?

As mentioned, the national Engineering World Health organization has a yearly design competition that we at TEWH compete in! In 2015, TEWH placed first in the Engineering World Health design competition and third in the National Institute of Health's Debut design competition with our project, Freepulse. There is still plenty of time to join one of TEWH's 2016-2017 design teams. No prior experience needed! In fact, it's our goal that you'll learn while working on the design competition. The design teams need curious members in many different fields ranging from business to hardware/software, EagleCAD design to proposal writing. If you're interested, there's a spot for you on one of our design teams.

Sensor Glasses

Visual impairment is defined as a level of vision that cannot be corrected back to a normal level even with glasses or other aids. Due to a number of factors, the vast majority of visually impaired persons are found in third world nations. Over 221 million people in the developing world suffer from visual impairment, and of that amount 31 million are completely blind. Many visually impaired persons in third world countries are also often unemployed and face socio-economic discrimination due to negative stigmas of their condition.

Traditionally, the visually impaired have relied on rudimentary aids such as the white cane in order to maneuver around their environment. Although useful, the white cane faces a number of serious limitations. The range is limited by arm length of the user and of the length of the cane itself. Also, users generally keep the cane low, leaving them vulnerable to objects above waist level. This prevents the user from having a full understanding of their surroundings. There has been some exploration in the field of ultrasonic canes; however, the average price of these device is over $600 and still have significant shortcomings. Economically disadvantaged visually impaired people do not have the means to afford such devices. Our team’s goal is to design a low-cost and effective solution to combat this problem of visual impairment.

FreePulse

FreePulse, a low-cost patient monitor, was the primary project of Texas Engineering World Health during the 2014-2015 academic year. Patient monitors play a crucial role in virtually all aspects of medical care, including emergency and surgical units, intensive and critical care units, and other non-critical units. Unfortunately, the cost of current market models ranges from $1,000 to $10,000, which has created a low patient-monitor-to-patient ratio in many developing countries.

There is a clear and demonstrated need for a low-cost patient monitor that can be purchased in bulk by developing world hospitals, thus giving many more patients the monitoring they need. Our project, the FreePulse low-cost patient monitor, aims to fill this gap in developing world healthcare and provide a durable, reliable, and accessible monitor.

FreePulse provides basic monitoring for heart rate, electrocardiogram signals (ECG), and percent saturated oxygen (SpO2) with analog and digital filtering. Visual alerts are implemented to warn nurses if a patient begins to experience cardiac or respiratory distress, matching the capability of current market devices. A replaceable backup battery and uninterrupted power circuit ensures that the device continues to function even in unstable power conditions.

This project was the winner of the Engineering World Health design competition and was the third place winner in the National Institue of Health's DEBUT competition.

Audiometry Made Easy

Audiometry Made Easy was the focus of the 2013-2014 Texas Engineering World Health chapter. The idea behind this project was to create a functional, free, open source mobile application to test hearing. This app performs a standard medical audiometry test, playing a series of tones over a range of 500 - 8000 Hz to determine hearing thresholds for both right and left ears.
